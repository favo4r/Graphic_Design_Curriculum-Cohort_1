Why This Program Is Different

Design Rooted in
African Visual Intelligence

Africa has one of the world's oldest traditions of visual communication — long before "graphic design" was named in the West. This program treats African visual systems not as decoration, but as a legitimate design education. You will study, deconstruct, and build on real African symbol systems as your first design projects.

Adinkra Symbols

Akan People · Ghana & Côte d'Ivoire

Over 100 symbols, each encoding a philosophical concept. Used in Weeks 1–4 to teach visual meaning, grid construction, and symbol design.

Kente Cloth

Ashanti & Ewe Peoples · Ghana & Togo

A modular grid system woven in silk and cotton. Each color and pattern carries cultural status. Used to teach color systems and pattern logic.

Nsibidi Script

Ejagham & Igbo Peoples · Nigeria & Cameroon

An ancient ideographic writing system with over 500 signs. Used to teach how visual language works before the alphabet.

Ndebele Murals

Ndebele People · South Africa & Zimbabwe

Bold geometric murals using strict color rules and mathematical precision. Used to teach color theory, contrast, and spatial composition.

Bogolan / Mudcloth

Bambara & Dogon Peoples · Mali

Textile symbols made with fermented mud, each with specific clan meaning. Used to teach texture, negative space, and pattern-as-identity.

Tifinagh Script

Amazigh (Berber) People · North Africa

One of the world's oldest alphabets, still in use today. Used to explore letterform geometry and the relationship between script and identity.

How it works in practice: In Weeks 1–4, you pick two African symbols that mean something to you. You study them, reconstruct them digitally, merge them into your own original pattern, and use that pattern to design your first professional poster. Your African design heritage becomes your creative foundation — not just a history lesson.

Unit A · Weeks 01–04 Design Foundations & African Visual Heritage
Foundation Phase

By Week 4 you will have designed your first professional poster using an original pattern built from scratch — rooted in an African symbol system you chose yourself.

Week 01

Design Foundations
+ African Design Heritage

Learn to see like a designer — then discover that African artists have been doing it for millennia.

identify the 7 design elements and core principles in real-world examples, and connect each principle to a specific African visual tradition

Before you open any software, you need to understand the rules that make design work. This week teaches those rules — then shows you they've been applied with mastery across Africa for thousands of years.

⬡ Elements of Design
  • What graphic design is and what designers actually do
  • Line — direction, weight, and emotion
  • Shape — geometric vs. organic forms
  • Form, space, texture, value, and color
  • How the 7 elements combine to create meaning
◎ Principles of Design
  • Contrast: making the important things stand out
  • Balance: symmetry, asymmetry, and visual weight
  • Alignment, repetition, and proximity (the A-R-P system)
  • Visual hierarchy: controlling what viewers see first
  • Unity and variety: consistency with intention
  • Gestalt: how the eye automatically groups elements
◑ African Design Heritage
  • Adinkra — 100+ symbols, each encoding a concept (Sankofa = "learn from the past")
  • Kente — a modular grid system woven in silk and cotton
  • Nsibidi — 500+ ideographic signs from Nigeria & Cameroon
  • Ndebele murals — geometric color systems, South Africa
  • Bogolan / Mudcloth — Mali textile symbol tradition
  • How each system is a direct application of design principles you just learned
◻ Visual Research Skills
  • How to study a visual reference — not just look at it
  • Symbol vs. pattern vs. motif: the critical distinction
  • Building a reference library and annotated mood board
  • Sketching to understand: drawing as a thinking tool
  • Starting your design sketchbook from Day 1
◈
Week 1 Milestone — Research & Selection

Research African patterns, symbols, and motifs from any tradition. Select two symbols or patterns that resonate with you meaningfully — not just visually. Document what each symbol means, where it comes from, and why you chose it. These two selections are the creative seed for everything you build in Weeks 2, 3, and 4.

  • Research document
  • 2 selected symbols
  • Written rationale
  • Annotated mood board
Week 02

Sketching, Adobe Illustrator
& Pattern Deconstruction

Pick up your first design tools and use them immediately to reconstruct your chosen African patterns from the ground up.

use Adobe Illustrator's core tools to digitally rebuild your two African patterns — proving you understand how they were constructed

Great designers sketch before they click. This week you learn to think with your hands first, then translate that thinking into Illustrator. Your first digital project starts on Day 1.

✏ Sketching & Ideation
  • Why every professional designer still sketches first
  • Thumbnail sketching: 10 ideas in 10 minutes
  • Grid-based construction sketching for patterns
  • Annotation sketching: drawing to understand, not just to plan
  • Translating a sketch into a digital construction brief
▲ Adobe Illustrator — Core Skills
  • Interface tour: artboards, panels, and tool bar
  • Shape tools: rectangle, ellipse, polygon, star, line segment
  • The Pen tool — Bezier curves from scratch
  • Stroke vs. fill: color, weight, and cap styles
  • Align and distribute objects with precision
  • Pathfinder: unite, minus front, intersect, exclude
  • Shape Builder: merging and subtracting shapes visually
◈
Week 2 Milestone — Deconstruct & Reconstruct

Deconstruct each of your two Week 1 symbols on paper — identify the base shapes, grid logic, and construction rules, then annotate your sketches. Then rebuild both patterns digitally in Illustrator. No tracing — reconstruction from understanding only. Goal: prove you know exactly how the pattern was made.

  • Annotated deconstruction sketches
  • 2 reconstructed Illustrator files
  • Construction process notes

Week 1 — your two selected African symbols

Week 03

Color Theory, Emotional Expression
& Creating Your Original Pattern

Learn the science and psychology of color — then use it to create an original pattern that belongs entirely to you.

merge your two reconstructed patterns into one original design and apply a purposeful color system that communicates a specific emotion

Color is the most emotionally immediate tool a designer has. Before the viewer reads a word, color has already told them how to feel. This week you learn to control that — and apply it to create your own signature pattern.

◑ Color Theory
  • Color models: RYB (mixing), RGB (screens), CMYK (print) — when each applies
  • Hue, saturation, value — the three variables you always control
  • Color harmonies: complementary, analogous, triadic, split-complementary
  • Warm vs. cool vs. neutral — emotional temperature in design
  • Color psychology: what each color communicates, culturally and universally
  • African color meaning: Kente gold = royalty, Ndebele red = transition, Maasai red = strength
  • How to build a 5-color palette from a single base hue
◈ Color in Adobe Illustrator
  • Global colors: change one swatch, update everything instantly
  • Creating, naming, and saving color palettes
  • Recolor Artwork: remap all colors across a complex illustration
  • Linear and radial gradients
  • Pattern fills and the live paint bucket
  • Exporting color-accurate files for print and screen
◈
Week 3 Milestone — Merge, Transform & Colorize

Merge your two Week 2 reconstructions into one original pattern — your own design. Find the visual tension, harmony, or conversation between the two source symbols. Apply a deliberate color system, and submit a short written rationale: what emotion does this communicate, and why did you make these color choices?

  • Original merged pattern (Illustrator)
  • 5-color palette document
  • Written color rationale

Week 2 — your two reconstructed patterns

Week 04

Applying Everything You've Learned
— Your First Poster

Bring together every principle, tool, and decision from Weeks 1–3 to produce your first complete, portfolio-ready piece.

design a finished, print-ready A3 poster featuring your original pattern — with clear hierarchy, deliberate typography, and a written design rationale

Every principle you have learned converges here. This is your first real design brief — with a specific message, a defined audience, and a standard you are expected to meet.

⬡ Poster Design Principles
  • Anatomy of a poster: focal point, hierarchy, supporting information
  • Directing the viewer's eye: entry point, flow, and exit
  • Contrast as your primary impact tool
  • Grid systems for poster layout: column and modular grids
  • White space: intentional breathing room, not empty space
  • How to use your pattern as a layout anchor without overwhelming the message
A Typography Basics
  • Type classification: serif, sans-serif, display, script — what each communicates
  • Font pairing: one display face + one body face
  • Type hierarchy: headline, subhead, body, caption
  • Leading, tracking, and kerning — precision spacing
  • Type as visual texture, not just information
  • Live text vs. outlined paths in Illustrator

Week 4 Major Project — Original Pattern Poster

The poster must communicate a specific message to a defined audience, use deliberate hierarchy, and apply your color system consistently. Submit a 1-paragraph design rationale explaining every key decision. Portfolio Project 1 of 5.

Unit B · Weeks 05–06 Print Design, Layout & Visual Communication
Tools & Application Phase

By Week 6 you will design a multi-page editorial publication for an African artist and understand how to control the message and tone of any layout.

Week 05

Print & Editorial Design
— Magazines, Brochures & InDesign

Design a multi-page print publication using Adobe InDesign — the professional standard for editorial layout.

set up a multi-page InDesign document with master pages, threaded text frames, and a consistent grid — then design a publication for an African artist

Editorial design is where information becomes an experience. You move from designing one surface to designing a sequence of pages — and learn to control how readers move through them.

▭ Editorial Design Theory
  • Magazine anatomy: cover, masthead, table of contents, feature spreads
  • Brochure formats: tri-fold, bi-fold, gatefold — and when each is appropriate
  • Pacing: how to control energy and rhythm across a sequence of pages
  • The spread rule: every two-page layout must work as one visual unit
  • Grid systems: 3-column, 6-column, 12-column — and when to use each
  • Pull quotes, sidebars, and captions — supporting the main story
◻ Adobe InDesign — Core Skills
  • Document setup: pages, margins, columns, bleed
  • Master pages: repeating headers, footers, and page numbers
  • Text frames: creating, linking, and threading across pages
  • Character and paragraph styles — consistent formatting across the whole document
  • Placing and cropping images in frames
  • Packaging and exporting: press PDF vs. screen PDF
★
Week 5 Project — Editorial Design for an African Artist

Design a 4–6 page magazine feature or bi-fold brochure for an African artist of your choice — visual artist, musician, filmmaker, or fashion designer. The design must visually reflect the artist's world, not just describe it. Required: a cover or outer panel, at least one feature spread with image and body copy, and a clear typographic hierarchy throughout. Portfolio Project 2 of 5.

  • InDesign file (packaged)
  • PDF export
  • Design rationale
Week 06

Visual Communication: Tone, Mode
& Layout Structure

Learn to set the mood and structure the flow of any design — so every layout communicates its message before a word is read.

diagnose why a layout is working or failing, apply a deliberate tone to any design brief, and restructure a poorly organized layout from scratch

Two weeks covering tone/mode and layout structure are combined here because they answer the same question: does this design communicate what it is supposed to? Tone controls the feeling; layout controls the flow. You need both at once.

◎ Mode, Tone & Visual Direction
  • Mode: which element carries the message — image, type, color, or form?
  • Tone: the 6 design tones — formal, playful, urgent, melancholic, bold, minimal
  • Visual direction: the feeling a design creates before anything is read
  • Semiotics basics: signs, symbols, and what images imply beyond what they show
  • Style tiles: testing visual tone before committing to a full layout
  • Designing the same brief for two different audiences — and seeing what changes
⊞ Layout Structure & Hierarchy
  • Z-pattern and F-pattern reading flow — and when each applies
  • Proximity: grouping elements to create relationships
  • Alignment: every element should align to an intention
  • Focal hierarchy: primary, secondary, and tertiary attention zones
  • Breaking the grid — the difference between intentional and accidental
  • Layout critique method: naming exactly what is failing and why
  • Exercise: redesign a badly structured layout in 30 minutes
Unit C · Weeks 07–08 Social Media, Digital Design & Publishing
Digital & Social Phase

By Week 8 you will design scroll-stopping social content and multi-frame digital sequences — and know exactly how to prepare any file for professional delivery.

Week 07

Designing for Social Media
— Strategy, Specs & Execution

Design platform-correct social content that stops the scroll — and understand the strategy behind why some content gets seen and most doesn't.

create a branded social media content system across 3 post formats using Photoshop and Canva, with correct specs and a written content strategy

Social media design is not graphic design made smaller. It has its own rules, attention spans, and performance logic. This week teaches you to design for platforms — not just aesthetics.

⊡ Social Media Design Theory
  • Why social design is different from print and web
  • Platform specs (exact px): Instagram, Stories, LinkedIn, YouTube thumbnail, TikTok
  • Feed vs. Stories vs. Reels — different design logic for each
  • The 3-second rule: what forces someone to stop scrolling
  • Content series: designing a system, not just single posts
  • Content pillars: how brands organize what they post
◰ Strategy & Research
  • Competitive audit: what's working in your niche and why
  • Designing for captions: visual and text must tell one story
  • Low-attention design: communicating in 2 seconds or less
  • Simple motion for social: GIFs and looping animations
▪ Tools — Photoshop & Canva
  • Photoshop Smart Objects: building reusable, updatable templates
  • Photo retouching essentials for social media
  • Canva: rapid production with brand kits and team features
  • Exporting correctly: PNG vs. JPEG, resolution for screens
  • Batch-exporting multiple platform sizes from one design
Week 08

Digital Sequencing, Figma
& Publishing to a Professional Standard

Integrated Week
Design a multi-frame digital sequence in Figma, then audit it against a professional refinement checklist before delivery.

build a cohesive 6-slide carousel in Figma, apply a full refinement audit, and prepare the files for professional client delivery

Sequencing and refinement are taught together because they are two stages of the same workflow — design it right, then finish it right. A carousel that isn't refined isn't ready. This week you do both, in order.

↺ Digital Sequencing
  • Sequencing logic: each frame must hook into the next
  • Visual continuity across frames: consistent type, color, and spacing
  • Pacing: when to simplify a slide vs. when to go dense
  • Cover design: the thumbnail is its own design problem
  • Infographic design: making data visual and scannable
◈ Figma — Introduction
  • Figma interface: frames, layers, and the properties panel
  • Auto-layout: frames that resize and adapt automatically
  • Components: reusable elements that update everywhere at once
  • Building a 6-slide carousel template from scratch
  • Testing your design on an actual mobile screen
⊹ Professional Refinement & Delivery
  • The refinement mindset: a design that looks "almost right" looks wrong
  • Spacing audit: consistent padding, margin, and rhythm on every frame
  • Alignment check: is every element intentionally placed?
  • Color consistency: global swatches — no rogue hex values
  • Typography check: hierarchy, leading, orphan lines, text rags
  • The thumbnail test: does it still read at 10% of its original size?
  • File hygiene and delivery: named layers, correct formats, client handoff note
Unit D · Weeks 09–11 Brand Identity, Systems & Client Delivery
Advanced Phase

Week 09

Brand Strategy, Logo Design
& Visual Language

Integrated Week
Develop a brand strategy and translate it directly into a logo, color palette, and typographic system — as a single connected workflow.

write a complete brand strategy brief, design a logo suite, select a brand typeface system, and define the visual language for your African startup

Strategy and logo design are taught in a single week because they are one workflow, not two separate tasks. Every logo decision must trace back to a strategy decision. Students who separate them produce decoration. Students who connect them produce design.

◉ Brand Strategy
  • Brand vs. logo — the distinction that changes how you design everything
  • Mission, vision, values — and how they constrain visual choices
  • Brand positioning: the single sentence that separates you from every competitor
  • Target audience: specific, not "everyone" — age, values, needs, context
  • Brand personality and archetypes: The Hero, The Creator, The Explorer, The Sage
  • African startup examples: Paystack, Flutterwave, Andela — what their brands communicate
  • Brand discovery document: the brief you complete before designing anything
◯ Logo Design
  • The 6 logo types and when to use each: wordmark, lettermark, pictorial, abstract, combination, emblem
  • Logo construction on a geometric grid
  • The 20-thumbnail rule — sketch 20 before opening Illustrator
  • Negative space and the mark of a great logo
  • The versatility test: black only, reversed, 16px, embroidered
  • Building a logo suite: primary, secondary, and icon variants
▣ Visual Language
  • Brand color palette: primary, secondary, accent, and neutral — each with a reason
  • The 60-30-10 rule: how to apply colors in correct proportion
  • Brand typeface selection: criteria beyond aesthetic preference
  • Type hierarchy for a brand: display, headline, body, caption, label
  • Iconography style: defining line weight, corner radius, and consistency rules
  • Photography direction: tone, subject matter, editing style

Your African startup selection — chosen this week as the brief for Weeks 9–11

Week 11

Brand Guidelines, Client Delivery
& the Complete Brand Project

Integrated Week
Document your brand as a set of rules any designer can follow, prepare a professional client delivery package, and submit your completed brand identity project.

produce a brand guidelines PDF, prepare a complete client delivery package, and submit a finished brand identity for an African startup as a portfolio project

Brand guidelines and client delivery are the same week because the guidelines document is the delivery. You are not done when you finish designing — you are done when your client can use the work without you in the room. That is the professional standard.

◰ Brand Guidelines Document
  • What brand guidelines are and why clients cannot function without them
  • Logo usage: correct use, incorrect use, minimum size, safe zone
  • Color specifications: HEX (digital), RGB (screen), CMYK (print), Pantone (spot)
  • Typography rules: which fonts for which contexts and sizes
  • Imagery and photography style guide
  • Tone of voice section: how the brand writes and speaks
  • Delivering as a designed PDF + organized Figma file
⊕ Professional Client Delivery
  • What belongs in a delivery package: source files, exports, guidelines, fonts
  • Print files: bleed, crop marks, color profile (CMYK vs. sRGB)
  • Digital files: correct resolution, format, and naming convention
  • Writing a clear client handoff note
  • Managing revision rounds professionally
  • The refinement checklist as a quality gate: spacing, alignment, color, type

Week 12

Portfolio, Freelancing

Graduation Week

launch a live portfolio with 4 case-studied projects, set your freelance rate, and deliver a 3-minute portfolio presentation to the class
